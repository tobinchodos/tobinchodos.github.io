Course Overview

Week One

This week we pick up where you left off with 120-B: late Beethoven. We also discuss Romantic aesthetics in general, the difference between "high" and "low," and begin our study of opera with some early 19th century Italian classics.

Week Two

This week, we deepen our study of Romanticism by looking at its Big versions and its Small versions. We discuss the rise of Lieder and musical miniatures, and the tendency toward literary and pictorial ambitions in music. We also discuss the importance of spectacle and virtuosity, and one of Romanticism's most enduring gifts to the modern era: the idea of "genius."

Week Three

You've heard of "warhorses" -- the constantly performed (and much-bemoaned) symphonic classics of the 19th century that still form much of today's standard repertoire. This week, we study some of the important ones, while also getting to know the idea at the core of the so called "war of the Romantics" -- we study the idea of absolute music, its philosophical roots, and its associated ideological polemics.

Week Four

This week is dedicated to some of the seminal works of two late 19th century Opera composers: Wagner and Verdi. Writing assignment #1 due 4/25

Week Five

Even though many classic Romantic works are symphonic, the 19th century actually saw a steady decline of activity in this genre. This week, we have only one day because of the midterm on 5/4. We will use it to examine the rise of the "symphonic style," focusing mostly on the music of Johannes Brahms.

Midterm Exam on Week 5, class 2 (5/4)

Week Six

This week, we begin the second phase of the course, transitioning from Romanticism to its closely related descendant, Modernism. We will focus on the ways in which the one carries on intellectual and aesthetic trends from the other, situating ourselves in two places: first, in Paris at the turn of the century, and then in New Orleans at the same time.

Week Seven

Modernism came, and continues to come, in many flavors. This week, we transition from the luxurious warmth of France and the African American diaspora to the exacting severity of Stravinsky and the 2nd Viennese school.

Writing assignment #2 due 5/18

Week Eight

This week, we continue our study of interwar music from Europe and North America, adding the music of Henry Cowell and Edgard Varese. We also discuss the unique musical modernism of Ruth Crawford and her relationship to Charles Seeger, revisiting the high-low divide with her attempts to join "dissonant counterpoint" and egalitarian political ideals.

Paper proposals due 5/25

Week Nine

Here is a frequently quoted line that Theodor Adorno wrote in 1949: "To write poetry after Auschwitz is barbaric." What did he mean? Did he have a point? This week, we look at three musical ways of confronting the aesthetic crisis that Adorno is referring to: Integral Serialism, Neoclassicism and an array of techniques that use randomness (chance methods, indeterminacy, etc). On day two of week nine, we study a contemporaneous African American trend: Bebop. These various threads seem disparate, but they actually have a lot in common.

Week Ten

We'll conclude by surveying the musical expressions of some idealists, collectives and utopians in the mid- to late-20th century United States. We'll ask whether music can be both activist and good, and whether or not it should try. Among others, we examine Cornelius Cardew, the AACM in Chicago, the Black Artists' Group. Also the Zulu Nation, Celeste Oram, and Ma'ayan Tsadka.
